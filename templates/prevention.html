What is a Malicious URL?

According to Cheap SSL Security, a malicious URL is a web address that has been created with the intention of causing harm to a user's computer or stealing their personal information. Some common examples of malicious URLs include phishing sites, malware-infected websites, and fake login pages. It's important to be vigilant when browsing the web and to always look for secure URLs when entering sensitive information.


Why cybercriminals may create malicious URLs ?

Cybercriminals may create malicious URLs to:

  • Carry out phishing attacks to gain access to users’ personal information to carry out identity theft or other types of fraud.
  • Gain access to users’ login credentials to gain access to their personal or professional accounts.
  • Trick users into downloading malicious software that cybercriminals can use to spy on victims or take over their devices.
  • Get into victims’ computers to encrypt their files for a ransomware attack.
  • Remotely control a victim’s computer by using a remote access trojan (RAT). And if an attacker can distribute RATs to other vulnerable devices on the network, they can use it to create a botnet that they can control.

How to Avoid Malicious URLs as an Individual User

You can do so by verifying in the following manner:

  • Check the Sender’s Email Address
  • Don’t Click on Random Pop-Ups or Ads
  • Check URL not beginning with https:// or shttp://; spelling and grammar mistakes in the link
  • URL filtering and Check the properties of any links.

How to protect against a malicious URL?

  1. Verify the website's certificate details:

    Even if the connection is secure, it's essential to confirm the identity of the website owner. Most browsers allow you to view the certificate by clicking on the padlock icon.

  2. Use online resources to detect malicious URLs:

    Websites like VirusTotal can analyze suspicious URLs using antimalware engines to identify URLs associated with malicious activity. Also, make sure that you have safe browsing checks enabled in your browser settings.

  3. Check the domain:

    You can leverage tools such as MX Toolbox and Who.is to identify the email address registered with the website and the registration date. These sites can help you evaluate the website for any red flags. Always treat new domains with suspicion as malicious domains are likely to have been registered very recently.

  4. Regularly update your systems:

    Cybercriminals often exploit vulnerable applications on devices. Regularly updating your operating systems, applications, and plugins provides better protection against malware.

  5. Educate yourself, your employees, and your customers:

    It's crucial to understand how cybercriminals use malicious URLs against organizations. Educate your staff through mandatory training sessions, email alerts, newsletters highlighting recent scams, and security concerns to mitigate risks. By taking these steps, you can minimize the risk of falling prey to malicious URLs.

Two Things to Look for in a Secure Website

According to IT@UW-Madison, there are two things to look for in a secure website:

  • Look at the uniform resource locator (URL) of the website.

    A secure URL should begin with “https” rather than “http.” The “s” in “https” stands for secure, which indicates that the site is using a Secure Sockets Layer (SSL) Certificate. This lets you know that all your communication and data is encrypted as it passes from your browser to the website’s server.

  • Look for a lock icon near your browser’s location field.

    The lock symbol and related URL containing “https” simply mean that the connection between your web browser and the website server is encrypted, which is important. It prevents others from eavesdropping or intercepting your communication between your browser and the website’s server. But don’t be fooled into thinking the website you’re going to is secure—it may not be.
